# Weekend Industries Brand Guidelines

Weekend Industries is a small group of friends who build useful software and ship it to people who might find it useful.

The brand should feel like a workshop with a sense of humor: precise, industrial, tactile, and a little strange in the best way. It can borrow the spirit of playful hardware design and small-batch product culture without imitating any one company too directly.

## Brand Idea

**Useful things from spare time.**

Weekend Industries exists because good ideas do not always need a company, a pitch deck, or a grand thesis. Sometimes a few people make a tool on a weekend, test it, refine it, and send it into the world.

The identity should make that process visible:

- Built by humans, not a faceless studio.
- Practical before polished.
- Small-batch, but not amateur.
- Technical, but warm.
- Whimsical, but never childish.
- Shipped, not endlessly theorized.

## Positioning

**Category:** small-batch software, tools, experiments, and utilities.

**Audience:** curious people who appreciate useful artifacts, careful details, honest constraints, and products that feel personal.

**Promise:** Weekend Industries makes things that are clear enough to use, sturdy enough to trust, and odd enough to remember.

**Operating mode:** prototype, improve, label, ship.

## Brand Pillars

### 1. Workshop Utility

Everything should feel useful. Pages, labels, apps, docs, and launch surfaces should look like they belong near a terminal, release checklist, issue tracker, debug panel, or workshop bench.

Use:

- Labels, codes, part numbers, dates, revision marks.
- Simple layouts with strong alignment.
- Visible systems: grids, tables, checklists, specs.
- Plain descriptions of what the thing does.

Avoid:

- Vague startup language.
- Decorative complexity.
- Fake luxury.
- Over-explaining the brand.

### 2. Friendly Precision

The work should look measured, but not sterile. The brand can use exact dimensions, modular grids, technical typography, and concise labels, then offset them with unexpected color, copy, or a small joke.

Use:

- Tight systems with one intentional irregularity.
- Manual-like instructions with human phrasing.
- Exact specs paired with casual notes.
- Buttons and controls that feel sturdy.

Avoid:

- Corporate minimalism.
- Chaotic scrapbook energy.
- Random hand-drawn decoration.

### 3. Small-Batch Proof

Weekend Industries should show signs of real making: version numbers, batch IDs, release notes, changelogs, build marks, issue stamps, and release cards.

Use:

- `BATCH 001`
- `REV A`
- `TESTED ON SATURDAY`
- `BUILT BY D / M / J`
- `FIELD NOTE`
- `SMALL RUN`

Avoid:

- Pretending every item is a giant product line.
- Hiding imperfections that make the work feel real.

### 4. Industrial Whimsy

The brand's personality comes from the contrast between machine-shop structure and playful detail.

Industrial:

- Flat colors.
- Hard edges.
- Mono text.
- Rules, rails, ticks, slots, labels.
- Release systems.

Whimsical:

- Odd color pairings.
- Tiny surprise copy.
- Friendly names for serious parts.
- Unexpected icons.
- Satisfying mechanical interactions.

## Name And Language

### Primary Name

Use **Weekend Industries** in prose.

Use **weekend.industries** when the brand appears as a mark, URL, handle, or product stamp inside an interface.

### Casing

Preferred:

- `Weekend Industries`
- `weekend.industries`
- `WI-001`
- `REV A`
- `Batch 003`

Avoid:

- `Weekend.Industries`
- `WEEKEND INDUSTRIES` as the default voice
- `Weekend Labs`
- `Weekend Studio`

### Tagline Options

Primary:

**Useful things from spare time.**

Secondary options:

- **Built on weekends. Shipped when useful.**
- **Software, tools, and other good ideas.**
- **Small-batch tools for practical people.**
- **Friends making useful software.**

### Voice

The voice is direct, observant, and lightly dry. It should sound like a capable friend explaining what they made.

Write like this:

- "A tiny tool for keeping track of the thing you keep forgetting."
- "Made in a short run. We may keep going if people use it."
- "This started as a weekend fix and became useful enough to ship."
- "No account required. No dashboard to admire. Just the thing."

Do not write like this:

- "Revolutionizing the future of productivity."
- "Empowering makers through next-generation innovation."
- "A premium ecosystem for modern creators."
- "Where passion meets disruption."

### Copy Rules

- Say what the thing does in the first sentence.
- Prefer verbs over adjectives.
- Use dry specificity instead of hype.
- Keep jokes small and optional.
- Never make the user feel like they joined a cult.

## Visual Direction

### Core Style

The visual language is **utility manual meets playful consumer electronics**.

It should feel like:

- A release label with taste.
- A workshop cabinet with color-coded drawers.
- A tiny operating manual for a strange but useful app.
- A release note from a small group that actually used the tool.

It should not feel like:

- A SaaS template.
- A fake factory brand.
- A toy company.
- A sterile engineering consultancy.

## Logo System

### Preferred Mark Direction

Use a compact system rather than a single decorative logo:

1. **Wordmark:** `weekend.industries`
2. **Stamp:** `WI`
3. **Index Mark:** `W/I`
4. **Product Code:** `WI-###`

The wordmark should be simple, lowercase, and typographic. The brand's distinctiveness should come from layout, spacing, stamp systems, color, and use, not from an overworked symbol.

### Logo Behavior

Use the mark like a manufacturer's label:

- Top-left on web interfaces.
- Bottom-right on launch graphics and release cards.
- Stamped on product detail pages.
- Embedded in app chrome, favicons, and loading states.
- Repeated as a tiny inspection mark.

### Logo Construction Notes

If a custom mark is designed later, it should follow these rules:

- Based on `W`, `WI`, or `W/I`.
- Built from simple geometric strokes.
- Usable at 16px.
- Works in one color.
- Has a technical or modular construction logic.
- Avoids mascots, crests, sparks, rockets, and generic maker icons.

## Color System

The palette should be practical, tactile, and slightly offbeat. Use neutral industrial surfaces with sharp accent colors.

### Core Colors

| Token | Hex | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Shop Black | `#151515` | Primary text, dark backgrounds, stamped marks |
| Bench Gray | `#D7D2C8` | Primary warm surface |
| Archive Tan | `#B99A6B` | Archive pages, dividers, utility panels |
| Safety Red | `#F04438` | Alerts, key calls to action, inspection marks |
| Signal Blue | `#2D7FF9` | Links, active states, technical highlights |
| Tool Yellow | `#F6C945` | Labels, tags, small emphasis |
| Fixture Green | `#2F8F5B` | Success states, shipped status, healthy systems |
| Paper White | `#F7F3EA` | Main page background |

### Color Ratios

- 60% warm neutral surface.
- 25% black, gray, and line work.
- 10% one strong accent.
- 5% surprise color.

Do not flood a page with every accent. Each product or release can pick one lead accent and one support accent.

### Color Pairings

Recommended:

- Paper White + Shop Black + Safety Red.
- Bench Gray + Shop Black + Signal Blue.
- Archive Tan + Shop Black + Tool Yellow.
- Shop Black + Paper White + Fixture Green.

Avoid:

- Full purple/blue tech gradients.
- Beige-only lifestyle palettes.
- Neon cyberpunk.
- Pastel toy-store palettes.

## Typography

Typography should be functional, condensed where useful, and readable at small sizes.

### Recommended Fonts

Primary sans:

- `IBM Plex Sans`
- `Inter`
- `Suisse Int'l` if available

Technical mono:

- `IBM Plex Mono`
- `JetBrains Mono`
- `Azeret Mono`

Condensed display:

- `IBM Plex Sans Condensed`
- `Archivo Narrow`
- `DIN Condensed` if available

### Type Roles

| Role | Style |
| --- | --- |
| Product title | Condensed sans, medium or semi-bold |
| Body copy | Plain sans, regular |
| Labels | Mono, uppercase, small |
| Specs | Mono, tabular numerals |
| Buttons | Sans or mono, compact |
| Notes | Sans, smaller, casual |

### Type Rules

- Use mono text for data, labels, codes, revisions, and timestamps.
- Use sentence case for human-facing copy.
- Use uppercase only for labels and stamps.
- Keep headings compact and grounded.
- Do not use wide letter spacing as decoration.
- Do not use oversized hero type unless the page is truly a launch page.

## Layout System

### Grid

Use a visible, practical grid:

- 4px base unit.
- 8px minimum spacing.
- 16px standard component spacing.
- 24px section spacing.
- 48px major page spacing.

Prefer layouts that feel arranged on a workbench:

- Side rails.
- Label columns.
- Spec tables.
- Product trays.
- Stacked panels.
- Index cards.
- Release rows.

### Shapes

- Corners: 2px to 6px.
- Borders: 1px solid.
- Buttons: rectangular, sturdy, not pill-shaped.
- Cards: flat, compact, no heavy shadows.
- Separators: visible rules, ticks, or rails.

### Texture

Use texture sparingly:

- Fine paper grain.
- Halftone dots.
- Light scanlines.
- Stamped ink effects.
- Subtle paper or archive fibers.

Texture should make software feel tactile, not dirty.

## Iconography

Icons should look like inventory symbols, instruction diagrams, or appliance controls.

Use:

- Simple line icons.
- Filled utility symbols for status.
- Arrows, boxes, knobs, sliders, bolts, brackets, tags, rulers, clocks.
- Icons inside square buttons.

Avoid:

- Cute mascot icons.
- Generic sparkle icons.
- Overly rounded app-store icon sets.
- Icons that need explanation to be useful.

## Illustration And Imagery

### Image Style

Images should show real interfaces, real prototypes, working sessions, or close-up details of the software.

Good subjects:

- Hands using or testing the software.
- Product interface detail shots.
- Labels, tape, bins, parts, tools.
- Screens photographed at an angle.
- Workbench surfaces.
- Release checklists, terminals, pull requests, and notes.
- Small diagrams.

Avoid:

- Abstract 3D blobs.
- Generic startup people.
- Stock-photo collaboration scenes.
- Dark blurred background images.

### Diagrams

Diagrams are part of the brand. Use them to explain:

- What the thing does.
- What changed between revisions.
- How to use it.
- What is included in the release.
- How the product is structured.

Diagram style:

- Thin black lines.
- Small numbered callouts.
- Simple arrows.
- Mono labels.
- Occasional accent color.

## UI Design Rules

Weekend Industries interfaces should feel like usable instruments.

### Components

Buttons:

- Rectangular.
- Clear labels.
- 1px border.
- Strong hover state.
- Use icons for common actions.

Inputs:

- Plain fields with clear labels.
- Mono text for technical values.
- No glowing borders.
- Error states should be direct and specific.

Cards:

- Use for repeated products, notes, releases, or files.
- Keep them flat.
- Include a label, status, date, or code when useful.

Navigation:

- Compact.
- Product-first.
- Avoid marketing-heavy nav.
- Use `Index`, `Tools`, `Notes`, `Releases`, `About` if needed.

### Interaction

Motion should be mechanical, not floaty:

- Snaps.
- Slides.
- Hinges.
- Ticks.
- Stepped transitions.
- Small spring only when it feels like a switch or button.

Avoid:

- Slow luxury fades.
- Floating cards.
- Constant ambient animation.
- Motion that makes tools harder to use.

## Product Page Pattern

Every shipped tool should have a consistent product page:

1. Product name.
2. One-sentence use case.
3. Status: prototype, live, private beta, paused, retired.
4. Product code.
5. Batch or revision.
6. What it does.
7. Key features.
8. How to use it.
9. Notes from the build.
10. Pricing, access, or availability, if relevant.

Example:

```text
WI-004 / Desk Pin
REV A / BATCH 001 / 2026-05

A tiny menu bar tool for remembering what you were doing before the meeting interrupted you.

Includes:
- Local notes
- Quick pin/unpin
- End-of-day cleanup

Built because context switching is expensive and browser tabs are bad memory.
```

## Release System

Releases should look like something from a small, organized software workshop. Weekend Industries does not need physical packaging; it needs recognizable launch wrappers, changelogs, release cards, app chrome, and documentation.

### Release Materials

- Product page.
- Changelog.
- Release card.
- Screenshot set.
- Short demo clip when useful.
- Docs or manual page.
- Tiny inspection mark in the footer or app chrome.

### Release Card Structure

Each release card should include:

- Brand mark: `weekend.industries`
- Product code: `WI-###`
- Batch number.
- Revision.
- Released date.
- Platform or stack.
- Short human note.

Example:

```text
weekend.industries

WI-007              REV B
BATCH 002           RELEASED 2026-05-07

APP: TAB DRAWER
PLATFORM: WEB / LOCAL STORAGE
NOTE: FOR THE TABS THAT KEEP MULTIPLYING
```

## Naming System

Product names should be plain but slightly charming.

Good patterns:

- Functional noun: `Tab Drawer`, `Clip Log`, `Pocket Checklist`.
- Utility phrase: `Done Box`, `Hold Button`, `Tiny Ledger`.
- Numbered artifact: `WI-003`, `Weekend Tool 006`.

Avoid:

- Faux futuristic names.
- Overly cute names.
- Names that hide what the thing does.
- Startup-style compound words.

## Content Types

### Tools

Finished or nearly finished software that people can use.

Required fields:

- Name.
- Code.
- Status.
- One-sentence use.
- Screenshots, demos, or diagrams.
- Availability.

### Notes

Build logs, decisions, and observations.

Style:

- Short.
- Specific.
- Honest about constraints.
- Include images, sketches, or tables when possible.

### Manuals

Instructions for shipped tools.

Style:

- Step-by-step.
- Plain language.
- Diagrams over paragraphs.
- Include troubleshooting.

### Changelogs

Every tool should have a changelog.

Example:

```text
REV B
- Renamed the export action.
- Made empty states clearer.
- Removed the unnecessary settings screen.
```

## Brand Applications

### Website

The website should behave like an index of useful software, not a landing page for an imaginary corporation.

First screen:

- Brand mark.
- One-line explanation.
- Current tools.
- Recent releases or notes.

Useful sections:

- `Tools`
- `In Progress`
- `Notes`
- `Archive`
- `About`

Avoid:

- Giant generic hero sections.
- Abstract value prop cards.
- Founder mythology.
- Empty claims about craft.

### Social Posts

Post like field notes:

- Product screenshot or interaction detail.
- Short caption.
- Batch or revision.
- What changed.
- Link to the thing.

Example:

```text
WI-006 / REV A

First small run of Tab Drawer is live.
Useful if your browser has three windows and none of them have a plan.
```

### Emails

Emails should be concise and practical.

Structure:

- What shipped.
- Who it is for.
- What changed.
- Link.
- Short note.

### Digital Badges

Badge styles:

- Inspection stamp.
- Batch label.
- Warning label with a tiny joke.
- Tiny product code.
- `BUILT ON A WEEKEND`
- `RUNS LOCALLY`
- `NO ACCOUNT`

Keep badges useful enough to communicate product behavior, not just decorate the page.

## Design Checklist

Before shipping a Weekend Industries page, tool, or asset, check:

- Does it say what the thing does quickly?
- Does it include a code, status, batch, or revision where useful?
- Does it feel built rather than decorated?
- Is there one small human detail?
- Are the colors restrained?
- Are the typography roles clear?
- Does the layout help someone inspect the thing?
- Would this still work in black and white?
- Would the release card make sense without extra explanation?
- Is the joke small enough that it does not become the product?

## Do And Do Not

Do:

- Make the useful part obvious.
- Show the real interface.
- Use systems, labels, and codes.
- Keep the tone human.
- Let small imperfections show when they prove the thing was made.

Do not:

- Pretend the group is larger than it is.
- Use vague innovation language.
- Over-polish the work until it loses personality.
- Copy any specific reference brand's layout or product language.
- Add whimsy that makes the product harder to understand.

## Working Assumptions

These guidelines assume Weekend Industries ships software only, with the website acting as a catalog, archive, release surface, and documentation hub. The system keeps the feeling of small-batch industrial design, but translates it into software-native artifacts: app chrome, release cards, changelogs, docs, status labels, screenshots, demos, and interface details.
